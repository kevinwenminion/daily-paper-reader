Interpretable AI-driven materiomics to decode microenvironmental cues for 34

Abstract 36 

Hydrogels that mimic the extracellular matrix create microenvironments 37 

containing diverse physicochemical cues that regulate stem cell fate, particularly their 38 

immunomodulatory and pro-regenerative functions. However, elucidating how 39 

microenvironmental cues regulate cell fate remains challenging because of complex 40 

multi-parameter interactions and nonlinear relationships, thereby requiring large 41 

numbers of samples and substantial experimental effort. Here, we present a42 

materiomics platform that integrates a large-scale systematic dataset with artificial 43 

intelligence (AI) to evaluate and predict optimal stem cell niche features for 44 

enhancing the immunomodulatory functions of mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs). 45 

Specifically, binary hydrogels composed of methacrylated alginate and gelatin 46 

(AMGM) were used to construct a comprehensive materiomics database 47 

incorporating seven critical physicochemical cues associated with MSC-mediated 48 

regulation of macrophage polarization. Machine learning models trained on this 49 

materiomics database successfully predicted hydrogel formulations that enhanced 50 

MSC immunomodulatory efficacy and further identified matrix stiffness as the 51 

physicochemical cue with the greatest contribution to MSC-mediated 52 

immunomodulation within the AMGM hydrogel system. In summary, this study 53 

demonstrates the utility of AI for evaluating the relative contributions of 54 

microenvironmental cues and establishes a data-driven research framework for 55 

investigating microenvironmental regulation of stem cell function. 56 

Keywords: Machine learning, Microenvironmental cues, Stem cell 57

1. Introduction 59 

Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) possess unique immunomodulatory and 60 

pro-regenerative capacities, making them a promising therapeutic strategy for treating 61 

inflammatory conditions in regenerative medicine [1]. Their ability to regulate 62 

immune responses, mainly through paracrine signaling, is now recognized as a major 63 

contributor to their therapeutic efficacy in numerous regenerative conditions [1, 2]. 64 

The unique immunomodulatory properties of MSCs have been previously 65 

demonstrated to mediate the proliferation, apoptosis, migration, and function of 66 

several immune cell types, such as macrophages, neutrophils, natural killer cells, T 67 

cells, and B cells [3]. Among these immune cells, macrophages are central effectors 68 

of the innate immune system and play critical roles in inflammation and tissue repair 69 

through dynamic transitions between pro-inflammatory (M1) and anti-inflammatory 70 

(M2) phenotypes in response to microenvironmental cues [4]. MSCs have been shown 71 

to promote tissue healing by attracting monocytes and macrophages and inducing 72 

macrophages to polarize towards the M2 phenotype [1], which also has a profound 73 

impact on the host inflammatory responses upon the implantation of external 74 

biomaterials [5]. Consequently, engineering the immune microenvironment to 75 

influence both stem cell and macrophage behavior has become a significant focus of 76 

regenerative medicine research. 77 

Hydrogels serving as biomimetic extracellular matrices (ECMs) can establish an 78 

engineered microenvironment for the culture and regulation of MSC functionalities, 79 

including preserving cell stemness and secretory capacity, improving cell survival and 80 

local retention, achieving targeted delivery efficiency, and allowing precise control of 81 

secretory profiles [6]. Due to the high degree of control over the physicochemical 82 

cues of hydrogels, they have been widely used as a platform for functional and 83

In recent years, materiomics has emerged as an interdisciplinary field that 116 

integrates materials science, genomics, data science, and artificial intelligence to 117 

accelerate biomaterial discovery [16]. Its central goal is to decode the relationships 118 

among material composition, structure, properties, and function through 119 

high-throughput experimentation, multidimensional data integration, and machine 120 

learning, thereby shifting biomaterial design from empirical trial-and-error 121 

approaches toward data-driven optimization [17, 18]. Existing AI-assisted strategies 122 

in biomaterial research can be broadly divided into two categories. One category uses 123 

high-dimensional biological data, such as transcriptomic profiles, to predict cell fate 124 

or differentiation outcomes. For example, the Deng group employed 12 transcriptomic 125 

datasets to predict MSC differentiation with 90% accuracy using a k-nearest neighbor 126 

classifier, enabling outcome prediction as early as day 7 [19]. Similarly, the Wang 127 

group used RNA-sequencing data collected from MSCs cultured in hydrogels to 128 

model myogenic and osteogenic differentiation, although the predictive performance 129 

of their random forest and linear regression models remained limited [20]. While 130 

these studies are valuable for early biological prediction, they mainly establish 131 

correlations between omics features and cell fate, and provide limited insight into how 132 

intrinsic biomaterial properties regulate the target function. The second category uses 133

To address this gap, the present study established an integrated materiomics 141

MSC-mediated immunomodulatory efficacy, but also to compare the relative 151 

contributions of multiple coupled matrix cues. In this way, the present work provides 152 

a material-property-function framework for investigating how intrinsic hydrogel 153 

properties regulate stem cell immunomodulation, while also reducing the 154

2. Results 162 

2.1 Establishment of a Materiomics Database Based on AMGM Hydrogel 163 

Matrices with Varying Compositions 164 

To establish a biocompatible and mechanically tunable microenvironment for 165 

mesenchymal stem cell immunomodulation, we selected a hybrid hydrogel system 166 

based on methacrylated alginate (AlgMA) and methacrylated gelatin (GelMA). Both 167 

alginate and gelatin are naturally derived polymers that have been extensively used in 168 

tissue engineering and cell microenvironment studies [22, 23]. GelMA provides ECM 169 

mimetic features, including intrinsic cell-adhesive motifs and matrix 170 

metalloproteinase-responsive characteristics, whereas AlgMA offers a readily 171 

processable and compositionally versatile polymer framework [22, 23]. After 172 

methacrylation, both components can be efficiently crosslinked to form hydrogel 173 

networks, thereby enabling systematic tuning of matrix stiffness and viscoelasticity. 174 

In addition, alginate-containing networks broaden the accessible mechanical design 175 

space, while the combination of AlgMA and GelMA enables the generation of 176 

relatively homogeneous hybrid matrices across a wide compositional range, making 177 

this system well suited for combinatorial matrix design. 178

To construct a photo-crosslinkable binary hydrogel system, we first synthesized 187 

AlgMA and GelMA by chemically modifying sodium alginate and gelatin with 188 

methacrylic anhydride. The two components were then crosslinked via free-radical 189 

photopolymerization under 365 nm UV irradiation (250 mW/cm², 60 s) using 0.2% 190

AlgMA and GelMA, showing characteristic absorption peaks at 1730 cm -1 and 1633 193

calculated DOM values were 47.7% for AlgMA and 85.3% for GelMA 197

compositions, precursor solutions containing 0.5-2.5% w/v AlgMA and 0.5-8% w/v 199 

GelMA were mixed and polymerized upon UV initiation. Three distinct states were 200

microenvironmental cues, thereby modulating MSC behavior and functions, including 217 

secretory activity [6]. From surface charge, chemical functional groups, and adhesive 218 

ligand presentation at the molecular level, to surface topography and pore size at the 219 

microstructural level, and ultimately to bulk mechanical properties at the 220 

macrostructural level, all these factors have been verified to influence MSC fate [22]. 221

charge, RGD spacing, hydrophilicity/hydrophobicity, pore size, matrix stiffness, 223

The presence of RGD sequences is crucial for cell adhesion, migration, and 234 

differentiation [9]. To investigate the role of RGD sequences in MSC 235 

immunomodulatory function, we utilized binary AMGM hydrogel systems in which 236

Pore size is an important structural feature of biomaterials that influences cellular 242 

and tissue responses in tissue engineering. Previous studies have demonstrated that 243 

different pore sizes can support distinct tissue regeneration requirements [24]. 244 

Specifically, pore sizes of around 5 μm promote neovascularization, those of 20-125 245 

μm facilitate skin regeneration, and those of 100-350 μm support bone regeneration 246 

[25]. In this study, the pore sizes of AMGM hydrogels were evaluated by scanning 247 

electron microscopy (SEM) imaging of lyophilized hydrogels. The analysis revealed 248 

pore sizes ranging from 4.64 to 22.95 μm. Moreover, pore size was negatively 249 

correlated with total polymer content. Specifically, formulations with lower polymer 250 

content exhibited larger pores, whereas increasing polymer content resulted in denser 251

research exploring the effects of hydrophilic/hydrophobic materials with MSC 255

advancements demonstrate that three dimensional (3D) culture of hematopoietic stem 257

maintains their cellular stemness [26]. However, the influence of material 259 

hydrophilic/hydrophobic on the immunomodulatory functions of MSCs remains 260 

largely unexplored. 261 

To address the research gap resulting from the limited number of studies 262 

investigating the relationship between material hydrophilicity/hydrophobicity and 263 

MSC immunomodulation, we performed contact angle measurements and found that 264 

the binary hydrogel matrices exhibited substantial variations in hydrophilicity, with 265 

contact angles ranging from 1.48° to 77.99°. These wide variations reflect the diverse 266 

hydrophilic and hydrophobic characteristics of hydrogels with different compositions 267 

(Figure 3E). 268 

Over the past decade, matrix stiffness has been widely recognized as a key 269 

mechanical parameter governing MSC fate by resisting cell-generated traction forces. 270 

However, many of these studies implicitly assume that the native MSC niche behaves 271 

as a purely elastic material. In reality, natural ECM, as well as most tissues and organs, 272 

exhibit viscoelastic behavior across multiple time scales [27, 28]. Unlike purely 273 

elastic materials, viscoelastic matrices dissipate applied forces, thereby altering how 274 

cell-generated traction forces are transmitted, stored, and remodeled within the 275 

microenvironment, as well as how external mechanical signals are conveyed to 276 

intracellular mechanotransduction pathways [12]. Therefore, investigations of MSC 277 

mechanobiology should consider both the elastic and viscoelastic properties of 278 

synthetic niches. 279

regulator of MSC fate, with soft matrices favoring neurogenesis, matrices of 281

subsequently undergoes time-dependent energy dissipation, a hallmark of viscoelastic 291

Therefore, viscoelasticity represents another important matrix property influencing 296

2.2 Quantitative Evaluation of MSC-Mediated Macrophage Polarization Using 322 

the AMGM Hydrogel Database 323 

To evaluate the suitability of AMGM hydrogels as functional 3D niches for MSC 324 

delivery, we investigated the biological behavior of living cells encapsulated within 325 

these matrices. Specifically, umbilical cord-derived mesenchymal stem cells 326 

(UCMSCs) were homogeneously suspended in precursor solutions and subsequently 327 

encapsulated through photopolymerization under UV irradiation (365 nm, 250 328 

mW/cm², 60 seconds) (Figure 4A). To systematically evaluate the cytocompatibility 329 

of the encapsulation process, live/dead fluorescent staining was performed. The 330 

results showed that UCMSCs maintained high viability across all selected hydrogel 331 

formulations after 48 h of culture (Figure 4C). To further evaluate cell proliferation, a 332 

CCK-8 assay was performed. The optical density values increased continuously over 333 

time, showing an approximately threefold increase between 24 and 72 h (Figure 4B), 334 

suggesting progressive cell growth within the hydrogel matrices. Together, these 335 

results indicate that the AMGM hydrogels provide a cytocompatible 336 

microenvironment that supports MSC survival and proliferation under the tested 337 

conditions. 338 

Beyond supporting cell viability, the engineered ECM also provides structural cues 339 

that regulate cell morphology and behavior. To evaluate the ability of UCMSCs to 340 

respond to the surrounding microenvironment, cytoskeletal staining was performed on 341 

cells encapsulated within different AMGM hydrogel formulations (Figure 4D). 342 

Through morphological analysis, we observed substantial differences in the 343 

cytoskeletal organization of UCMSCs encapsulated within different hydrogel 344 

formulations [7]. MSCs cultured in hydrogels with lower polymer content exhibited a 345 

more spread morphology with extensive cellular protrusions, whereas those 346 

encapsulated in higher-polymer-content hydrogels appeared more rounded. 347

2.3 Virtual Database Construction and Screening of AMGM Hydrogels for 416 

MSC-Mediated Macrophage Polarization 417 

To identify AMGM formulations that maximize MSC-mediated 418 

immunomodulatory capacity, regression models were established to correlate 419 

hydrogel composition with the macrophage polarization index, and the 420 

best-performing model was subsequently selected for virtual database construction 421 

and formulation screening (Figure 6A) [39]. Six algorithms were evaluated, including 422 

decision tree regression (DT), Gaussian regression (GR), support vector machine 423 

regression (SVM), least squares boosting regression (LSBoost), multilayer perceptron 424 

(MLP), and generalized additive model regression (GAM) [40-43]. 425 

Among these models, all except SVM achieved overall prediction accuracies above 426 

90%. LSBoost showed the best predictive performance, with R² = 0.998 and a 5K 427 

MAE mean = 0.064 (Figure 6B). Accordingly, LSBoost was selected as the baseline 428 

model for subsequent optimization. Its superior performance is likely attributable to 429 

the ability of LSBoost to capture complex nonlinear relationships and improve 430 

robustness to dataset noise through residual-based optimization, making it well suited 431 

for modeling composition–function relationships within the AMGM hydrogel system 432 

[44]. 433 

We next optimized the LSBoost hyperparameters using Bayesian optimization 434 

together with 30 intelligent optimization algorithms. Among these approaches, 435 

particle swarm optimization (PSO) produced the best overall performance, yielding R² 436 

= 0.999 and a 5K MAE mean = 0.045 (Figure 6C, Supplementary Figure 4). The 437 

PSO-optimized LSBoost model was therefore selected as the final predictive model. 438 

The superior performance of PSO is likely attributable to its global search capability, 439 

which enables comprehensive exploration of the multidimensional parameter space 440 

and reduces the risk of convergence to local optima [45]. 441

2.5 SHAP-Based Feature Importance Ranking of Physicochemical Cues 617 

Governing MSC Immunomodulatory Capacity 618 

To establish quantitative relationships between physicochemical properties of the 619 

hydrogel matrix and MSC-mediated immunomodulatory function, a620 

machine-learning-based feature importance ranking framework was constructed using 621 

the physicochemical characteristics of the AMGM hydrogels and the corresponding 622 

macrophage polarization index data (Figure 9A; Supplementary Figure 8). First, 623 

recursive feature elimination with cross-validation (RFECV) was applied to evaluate 624 

the seven physicochemical features. The results indicated that pore size exhibited 625 

limited predictive value and was therefore excluded from subsequent analyses (Figure 626 

9B). Subsequently, predictive models were trained using the remaining six 627 

physicochemical features together with the corresponding biological output data. 628 

Multiple regression algorithms, including DT, GR, SVM, LSBoost, MLP, and GAM, 629 

were evaluated. For each algorithm, the dataset was divided into training, validation, 630 

and test subsets for model optimization and performance assessment. Among all 631 

tested models, the multilayer perceptron (MLP) exhibited the best predictive 632 

performance, achieving R 2 = 0.983 and a 5K MAE mean = 0.108 (Figure 9C). 633 

Accordingly, the MLP model was selected as the baseline model for subsequent 634 

analyses. 635

model was subjected to systematic hyperparameter optimization after being selected 637 

as the baseline model. Hyperparameter tuning was performed using Bayesian 638 

optimization in combination with 30 intelligent optimization algorithms, and the 639 

performance of each approach was evaluated using 5-fold cross-validation [47]. The 640 

results showed that Bayesian optimization achieved the best overall performance, 641 

yielding R² = 0.984 and a 5K MAE mean = 0.093 (Figure 9D). 642

3. Discussion 751 

The biological performance of biomaterials used in tissue engineering is a critical 752 

determinant of regenerative outcomes, as local physicochemical cues within the 753 

extracellular microenvironment exert profound effects on the behavior of MSCs and 754 

immune cells [11]. However, because microenvironmental regulation is inherently 755 

complex, multidimensional, and nonlinear, conventional trial-and-error approaches 756 

often require extensive experimental effort and become increasingly inefficient as the 757 

number of variables increases. This challenge is particularly evident in the 758 

development of MSC-laden hydrogels, where both material and biological 759 

requirements must be considered simultaneously. On the one hand, the biomaterial 760 

must possess properties suitable for implantation, including biodegradability, 761 

processability, and low immunogenicity. On the other hand, the hydrogel 762 

microenvironment must support cell viability while maintaining the desired biological 763 

function. 764 

A key translational contribution of the present study lies not only in the 765 

identification of an optimized hydrogel formulation but also in the establishment of a 766 

predictive framework linking material composition, physicochemical properties, and 767 

biological function. Through systematic in vitro and in vivo validation, we 768 

demonstrated that the machine-learning framework could reliably predict 769

Forward design in the interdisciplinary fields of machine learning and polymer 774
